Understanding Georgia State Income Tax

Georgia's income tax system has transitioned to a flat tax rate of 5.49% for tax year 2026, continuing the state's multi-year plan to simplify its tax structure. Previously, Georgia used a progressive tax bracket system with rates ranging from 1% to 5.75%.

This flat tax applies to all Georgia taxable income after deductions and exemptions, making it easier for taxpayers to estimate their state tax liability. The simplification benefits both individual taxpayers and businesses operating in Georgia.

Georgia Standard Deductions for 2026

  • Single: $12,000
  • Married Filing Jointly: $24,000
  • Married Filing Separately: $12,000
  • Head of Household: $16,000

Georgia's standard deductions are tied to federal standard deductions, which adjust annually for inflation.

Retirement Income Exclusions

Georgia offers generous tax benefits for retirees:

  • Social Security benefits: Completely exempt from Georgia state income tax
  • Retirement income exclusion: Up to $65,000 per person (age 65+) or $130,000 for couples
  • Eligible income: Includes pensions, 401(k) distributions, IRA withdrawals, and other retirement income

This makes Georgia one of the most tax-friendly states for retirees, particularly those with substantial retirement savings.

Who Must File a Georgia Tax Return?

You must file a Georgia income tax return if:

  • You are a Georgia resident or part-year resident with income
  • Your gross income exceeds the standard deduction for your filing status
  • You are a non-resident with Georgia-source income (from employment, business, or property in Georgia)
  • You want to claim a refund of taxes withheld

Georgia Tax Credits and Deductions

Georgia offers various tax credits that can reduce your tax liability:

  • Child and Dependent Care Credit: Up to $600 per child (income limits apply)
  • Low Income Credit: For taxpayers with adjusted gross income below certain thresholds
  • Adoption Credit: For qualified adoption expenses
  • Rural Physician Tax Credit: For physicians practicing in rural Georgia counties
  • Film/Entertainment Tax Credit: For qualified production expenditures (Georgia is a major film production hub)

Important Tax Dates for Georgia Filers

  • April 15, 2027: Deadline for filing 2026 Georgia income tax returns
  • Quarterly estimates: April 15, June 15, September 15, 2026, and January 15, 2027
  • Extension deadline: October 15, 2027 (with approved extension)

How Georgia Tax Compares to Other States

Georgia's 5.49% flat tax rate is competitive compared to neighboring states:

  • Florida: No state income tax
  • Tennessee: No state income tax (as of 2021)
  • Alabama: 2% to 5% progressive brackets
  • South Carolina: 0% to 6.5% progressive brackets
  • North Carolina: 4.5% flat tax

While Georgia doesn't match Florida and Tennessee's zero income tax, its flat rate is simpler than many neighboring states and competitive with North Carolina.

Georgia Local Taxes

In addition to state income tax, Georgia residents pay local sales taxes and property taxes:

  • State sales tax: 4%, with local rates adding 3-5% (total typically 7-9%)
  • Property tax: Varies by county; average effective rate is 0.87% of home value
  • No local income tax: Unlike some states, Georgia does not allow counties or cities to impose local income taxes

Tips to Lower Your Georgia Tax Bill

  • Maximize retirement contributions: 401(k) and traditional IRA contributions reduce your federal AGI, which Georgia uses as its starting point
  • Claim all eligible deductions: Georgia allows most federal deductions, including mortgage interest and charitable contributions
  • Use tax credits: Don't overlook Georgia-specific credits like the child care credit or low-income credit
  • Consider retirement location: If you're 65+, the $65,000/$130,000 retirement income exclusion can significantly reduce your tax burden
  • Timing income: Since Georgia uses a flat rate, bunching income into one year doesn't trigger higher brackets like in progressive tax states
