Understanding HELOCs (Home Equity Lines of Credit)

A HELOC is a revolving credit line secured by your home's equity. Unlike a traditional home equity loan that gives you a lump sum, a HELOC works more like a credit card—you can borrow, repay, and borrow again up to your credit limit during the draw period. HELOCs typically have two distinct phases:

Draw Period (Typically 5-10 Years)

During this phase:

  • Borrow as needed: Access funds up to your credit limit whenever you need them
  • Interest-only payments: Most HELOCs only require interest payments during this period, keeping monthly costs low
  • Revolving credit: As you pay back principal, that amount becomes available to borrow again
  • Variable rates: Most HELOCs have variable interest rates tied to the Prime Rate, which can fluctuate monthly

Example: With a $100,000 HELOC at 8.5% APR, if you draw $50,000, your monthly interest-only payment would be approximately $354. Your balance remains $50,000 throughout the draw period if you only make minimum payments.

Repayment Period (Typically 10-20 Years)

After the draw period ends:

  • No more borrowing: The line of credit closes—you can no longer draw funds
  • Principal + interest: Payments increase significantly as you now pay both principal and interest
  • Fixed schedule: Amortized payments over the remaining term to fully pay off the balance
  • Payment shock: Many borrowers are surprised by how much payments increase in this phase

Continuing the example: That $50,000 balance with $354/month payments during the draw period would jump to approximately $429/month during a 20-year repayment period at 8.5% APR—a 21% increase. Total interest over 30 years would be around $55,000.

HELOC vs. Home Equity Loan: What's the Difference?

HELOC (Line of Credit):

  • Revolving credit—borrow multiple times up to your limit
  • Variable interest rate (typically Prime + margin)
  • Interest-only payments during draw period
  • Flexibility to borrow only what you need, when you need it
  • Good for: Ongoing expenses like home renovations, college tuition, or emergency fund

Home Equity Loan (Lump Sum):

  • One-time lump sum disbursement
  • Fixed interest rate for the life of the loan
  • Fixed monthly payments from day one
  • Predictable payment schedule and total cost
  • Good for: One-time large expenses like a major renovation, debt consolidation, or business investment

How Much Can You Borrow with a HELOC?

Most lenders allow you to borrow up to 80-85% of your home's value minus your mortgage balance. This is called your Combined Loan-to-Value (CLTV) ratio.

Calculation example:

  • Home value: $400,000
  • Current mortgage balance: $250,000
  • Maximum CLTV: 85% = $340,000
  • Available equity: $340,000 - $250,000 = $90,000 HELOC limit

Some lenders offer 90% CLTV or even 100% for borrowers with excellent credit, but these come with higher rates and stricter requirements.

Current HELOC Interest Rates & How They're Set

HELOC rates are typically variable and based on the Prime Rate plus a margin (usually 0% to 3%). As of early 2026, the Prime Rate is around 7.50-8.50%, meaning most HELOCs are in the 7.5-11% range depending on:

  • Credit score: 740+ gets the best rates; below 680 faces higher margins
  • CLTV ratio: Lower ratios (more equity) qualify for better rates
  • Debt-to-income ratio: Lenders prefer DTI below 43%
  • Lender type: Credit unions often offer 0.25-0.5% lower rates than banks

Rate caps: Most HELOCs have lifetime rate caps (e.g., 18% maximum) and periodic caps (e.g., no more than 2% increase per year). Check your loan documents for these protections.

HELOC Fees & Closing Costs

Many lenders advertise "no closing cost" HELOCs, but there are usually conditions. Typical fees include:

  • Appraisal fee: $300-$600 (sometimes waived for high equity)
  • Application/origination fee: $0-$500
  • Title search and insurance: $500-$1,500
  • Annual fee: $0-$100/year (some lenders charge this)
  • Inactivity fee: $50-$100 if you don't use the line for 12+ months
  • Early closure fee: $300-$500 if you close within 2-3 years (to recoup waived costs)

Total closing costs typically range from $500 to $3,000. If the lender waives these, read the fine print—there may be a higher interest rate or early closure penalty.

Strategic Uses for a HELOC

1. Home improvements with added value: Renovations that increase home value (kitchen, bath, addition) can pay for themselves when you sell. Interest may be tax-deductible if used for home improvements.

2. Debt consolidation: If you have high-interest credit card debt (18-25% APR), consolidating with a HELOC at 8-10% saves significant interest. However, you're converting unsecured debt to secured debt—defaulting means risking your home.

3. Emergency fund backup: Keep a HELOC open with zero balance as a financial safety net. Access it only for true emergencies (job loss, medical expenses). No cost if you never draw on it.

4. Investment opportunities: Some investors use HELOCs to fund real estate purchases, business ventures, or stock investments. This is high-risk—if the investment fails, you still owe the debt secured by your home.

5. College tuition: HELOC rates are often lower than Parent PLUS loans (currently 8-9%). However, HELOC interest is generally NOT tax-deductible for education, while student loan interest may be (up to $2,500/year).

Risks and Drawbacks of HELOCs

Payment shock: Borrowers who only make interest-only payments during the draw period often face 30-50% payment increases when the repayment period begins. Plan ahead—make principal payments during the draw period if possible.

Variable rates: If the Prime Rate rises, your monthly payment increases. A 2% rate increase on a $50,000 balance adds about $83/month to your payment. Use our calculator to model different rate scenarios.

Home as collateral: Unlike credit card debt, a HELOC is secured by your home. If you can't make payments, the lender can foreclose. Never borrow more than you can comfortably repay.

Declining home values: If your home value drops significantly, lenders can freeze or reduce your credit line. This happened to many homeowners during the 2008 financial crisis.

Overborrowing temptation: Easy access to funds can lead to unnecessary spending. Treat a HELOC like a tool, not free money. Have a specific purpose and repayment plan before drawing funds.

Tax Deductibility of HELOC Interest

Under current tax law (Tax Cuts and Jobs Act), HELOC interest is tax-deductible ONLY if you use the funds to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the home securing the loan. The deduction is limited to interest on $750,000 of combined mortgage and HELOC debt ($375,000 if married filing separately).

Deductible: Using HELOC to add a room, remodel a kitchen, or replace a roof

NOT deductible: Using HELOC to pay off credit cards, buy a car, fund a vacation, or pay college tuition

Consult a tax professional to understand your specific situation. Keep detailed records of how you use HELOC funds.

Frequently Asked Questions

What credit score do I need to qualify for a HELOC?
Most lenders require a minimum credit score of 620-640 to qualify for a HELOC, but you'll need 700+ for competitive rates and 740+ for the best rates. Credit unions may be more flexible with credit score requirements than traditional banks. If your score is below 620, consider a personal loan or improving your credit before applying.
How does a HELOC payment change over time?
During the draw period (typically 10 years), most HELOCs require interest-only payments, which are relatively low. For example, a $50,000 balance at 8.5% APR costs about $354/month. When the repayment period begins, payments jump significantly as you now pay principal + interest. That same $50,000 balance would cost around $429/month over 20 years—a 21% increase. Additionally, since HELOC rates are variable, your payment can change monthly based on the Prime Rate.
Can I pay off a HELOC early without penalty?
Most HELOCs allow early payoff without prepayment penalties. However, some lenders charge an "early closure fee" ($300-$500) if you close the HELOC within 2-3 years. This fee helps the lender recoup closing costs they waived upfront. Check your loan documents for specific terms. You can always pay down the balance without closing the line—this avoids fees and keeps the credit available as an emergency fund.
What happens if I don't use my HELOC?
If you don't draw on your HELOC, you typically pay nothing—no interest, no monthly payments. Some lenders charge a small annual fee ($50-$100) or an inactivity fee if you don't use the line for 12-24 months. Keeping a HELOC open with a zero balance is a smart financial safety net. However, the credit line may count against your debt when applying for other loans, potentially affecting your borrowing capacity.
How is a HELOC different from a home equity loan?
A HELOC is a revolving line of credit (like a credit card) that you can borrow from repeatedly during the draw period. It typically has a variable interest rate and interest-only payments initially. A home equity loan gives you a lump sum upfront with a fixed interest rate and fixed monthly payments from day one. HELOCs are better for ongoing or uncertain expenses (renovations over time, emergency fund), while home equity loans are better for one-time large expenses with predictable costs (debt consolidation, major purchase).
What happens to my HELOC if home values drop?
If your home value declines significantly, your lender can freeze or reduce your HELOC credit limit to maintain required loan-to-value ratios. This happened widely during the 2008 housing crisis. For example, if you have a $100,000 HELOC but have only drawn $30,000, and your home value drops 20%, the lender might reduce your limit to $50,000—you'd still owe $30,000 but couldn't borrow the remaining $70,000. Lenders review home values periodically, especially in declining markets.
Is HELOC interest tax deductible?
HELOC interest is tax-deductible ONLY if you use the funds to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the home that secures the loan. Using a HELOC for home renovations, additions, or major repairs qualifies for the deduction (up to $750,000 of combined mortgage and HELOC debt). Using a HELOC for debt consolidation, car purchases, vacations, or education does NOT qualify for the deduction. Keep detailed records of how you spend HELOC funds and consult a tax professional for your specific situation.
Should I get a fixed-rate or variable-rate HELOC?
Most HELOCs are variable-rate by default (tied to Prime Rate), but some lenders offer a fixed-rate option where you can convert all or part of your balance to a fixed rate for a set period. Variable rates are lower initially but carry interest rate risk—if the Prime Rate rises, your payment increases. Fixed-rate options provide payment stability but typically have higher starting rates (0.5-1% more). If you expect to carry a balance for several years and rates are currently low, consider locking in a fixed rate. If you plan to pay off quickly (1-2 years), variable rates are usually cheaper.
