Understanding New Jersey Income Tax

New Jersey operates a progressive income tax system with six tax brackets ranging from 1.4% to 10.75%. The state has one of the highest top marginal rates in the nation, second only to California and Hawaii. Combined with the nation's highest property taxes, New Jersey is consistently ranked as one of the most heavily taxed states in America.

Key Features of New Jersey Tax System

  • No Standard Deduction: Unlike most states, NJ doesn't offer a standard deduction. You must itemize or use NJ-specific deductions.
  • High Top Rate: The 10.75% rate is the second-highest in the US (after California's 13.3%).
  • Millionaire's Tax: The top two brackets (8.97% and 10.75%) only affect high earners ($500k+).
  • No Social Security Tax: Social Security benefits are fully exempt from state tax.
  • Pension Exclusion: Up to $100,000 of pension/retirement income can be excluded for taxpayers age 62+ with income under $150,000.
  • Property Tax Deduction: You can deduct up to $15,000 of property taxes paid (NJ property taxes are the highest in the nation, averaging $9,284/year).

NYC Commuters: The NJ Tax Trap

Many people live in New Jersey and commute to New York City for work. Here's how taxation works:

  • You pay NJ tax on all income as a resident, regardless of where you work
  • You also pay NYC/NY tax if you work in NYC (NY withholds from your paycheck)
  • You get a credit on your NJ return for taxes paid to NY/NYC, but it doesn't always fully offset
  • Result: NJ residents working in NYC often pay more total tax than NY residents due to high NJ rates + property taxes

Example: A $200,000 earner living in Bergen County, NJ and working in Manhattan pays:

  • NY state tax: ~$11,000
  • NYC tax: ~$8,000
  • NJ tax: ~$9,200 (after credit for NY taxes paid)
  • NJ property tax: ~$15,000 (Bergen County average)
  • Total tax burden: ~$43,200 (21.6% of income)

Tax Planning Strategies for New Jersey Residents

1. Max Out Retirement Contributions

Traditional 401(k) and IRA contributions reduce your NJ taxable income. At the 6.37% marginal rate (most middle-income earners), a $23,000 401(k) contribution saves $1,465 in state tax. At the 10.75% top bracket, it saves $2,472.

2. Claim the Property Tax Deduction

You can deduct up to $15,000 of property taxes paid on your NJ return. Since NJ property taxes average $9,284 (and exceed $20,000 in many wealthy towns), this deduction is valuable. At 6.37%, deducting $15,000 saves $955 in state tax.

3. Retirees: Claim the Pension Exclusion

If you're 62+ with income under $150,000, you can exclude up to $100,000 of pension, 401(k), and IRA income. This is in addition to the Social Security exemption. A couple both 62+ could potentially exclude $200,000 of retirement income from state tax.

4. Consider Relocating After Retirement

Many NJ retirees move to Florida (0% income tax, lower property tax) or Pennsylvania (3.07% flat rate, much lower property tax). Moving from NJ to FL can save a high-income retiree $10,000-$20,000/year in taxes.

5. Strategic Timing for Bonuses/Capital Gains

If you're near the $500,000 (single) or $1 million (married) threshold, consider deferring bonuses or capital gains to avoid the 8.97% or 10.75% top brackets. Conversely, if you expect higher income next year, accelerate income into the current year.

6. College Savings: NJBEST 529 Plan

New Jersey offers a 529 plan (NJBEST) but does NOT provide a state tax deduction for contributions (unlike most states). However, withdrawals for qualified education expenses are tax-free at both federal and state levels.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the New Jersey state income tax rate for 2026?
New Jersey uses a progressive income tax system with six brackets ranging from 1.4% to 10.75%. The top rate of 10.75% applies to income over $1 million for married couples and $500,000 for single filers, making it one of the highest state tax rates in the country.
Does New Jersey tax Social Security benefits?
No, New Jersey does not tax Social Security retirement benefits. Additionally, NJ offers a pension exclusion of up to $100,000 for taxpayers age 62+ with income under $150,000, making it relatively tax-friendly for retirees despite high rates on working income.
Does New Jersey have a standard deduction?
No, New Jersey does not offer a standard deduction like most states. Instead, the state allows certain exemptions and deductions for dependents, property taxes (up to $15,000), and other specific items. This means you must itemize or use NJ-specific deductions.
Is New Jersey a high-tax state?
Yes, New Jersey has one of the highest state income tax burdens in the US. The top rate of 10.75% is second only to California (13.3%) and Hawaii (11%). Combined with high property taxes (highest in the nation), NJ is consistently ranked as one of the most heavily taxed states.
When are New Jersey state tax returns due?
New Jersey state tax returns are due on April 15th, the same deadline as federal returns. If you file a federal extension to October 15th, New Jersey automatically grants you an extension to the same date. However, any tax owed must still be paid by April 15th to avoid penalties.
