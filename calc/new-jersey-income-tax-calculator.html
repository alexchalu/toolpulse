Understanding New Jersey Income Tax

New Jersey operates one of the most progressive income tax systems in the United States, with six tax brackets ranging from 1.4% to 10.75%. The state's top rate is among the highest nationally, applying only to very high earners ($1 million+ for single filers, $5 million+ for married couples).

Exemptions (Instead of Standard Deduction)

New Jersey does not have a standard deduction. Instead, it offers personal exemptions that phase out at higher income levels:

  • Single / Married Filing Separately: $1,000 exemption (phases out for income over $100,000)
  • Married Filing Jointly / Head of Household: $2,000 exemption (phases out for income over $150,000)
  • Dependents: $1,500 per dependent (phases out at same thresholds)
  • Age 65+ or Blind: Additional $1,000 exemption per taxpayer

Key Features of New Jersey Tax System

  • Very Progressive: The bottom 50% of earners pay an average 1-2% rate, while top earners pay 8-10%+.
  • No Social Security Tax: Social Security benefits are completely exempt from state tax.
  • Pension Exclusion: Taxpayers with income under $100,000 (single) or $150,000 (married) can exclude up to $100,000 of pension/retirement income.
  • Property Tax Deduction: Unlike federal SALT cap, NJ allows full property tax deduction on state returns (most homeowners pay $8,000-15,000/year).
  • Millionaire's Tax: The 10.75% top rate is specifically designed to tax very high earners.

NYC Commuters: Where Should You Live?

Many people work in Manhattan but live in NJ, NY, or CT. Here's the tax impact:

  • Live in NJ, Work in NYC: You pay NJ income tax + NYC payroll tax (0.34% for metro commuter tax). You DON'T pay NYC income tax (only NYC residents pay that).
  • Live in NYC, Work in NYC: You pay NY state (4-10.9%) + NYC (3.078-3.876%) = up to 14.8% combined.
  • Tax Savings Example: A $200,000 earner in NJ pays ~$8,500 state tax. The same earner in NYC pays ~$16,000 (state + city). That's $7,500/year savings, but NJ property taxes are $3,000-5,000 higher, so net benefit is ~$2,500-4,500/year.

Why People Leave (and Stay) in New Jersey

Reasons to leave:

  • High property taxes ($9,284 average — highest in US)
  • High income tax (10.75% top rate for millionaires)
  • High cost of living overall
  • States like Florida (0% income tax) offer $15,000-30,000/year in tax savings for high earners

Reasons to stay:

  • Proximity to NYC jobs (45-minute commute vs. relocation)
  • Excellent public schools (justifies high property taxes for families)
  • Generous pension exclusion for retirees ($100k exempt)
  • No Social Security tax (saves $2,000-4,000/year for retirees)

Tax Planning Strategies for New Jersey Residents

1. Maximize Retirement Contributions

Traditional 401(k) and IRA contributions reduce both federal and NJ taxable income. At the 6.37% bracket (most common for middle-class earners), a $23,000 401(k) contribution saves $1,465 in NJ tax + ~$5,060 federal = $6,525 total.

2. Claim the Pension Exclusion

If you're retired with income under $100,000 (single) or $150,000 (married), you can exclude up to $100,000 of pension, IRA, and 401(k) distributions. This is one of the most generous retirement tax breaks in the country. A retiree with $80,000 in pension income would owe $0 NJ state tax.

3. Deduct Full Property Taxes

Unlike the federal $10,000 SALT cap, New Jersey allows unlimited property tax deductions on your state return. If you pay $12,000/year in property taxes and you're in the 6.37% bracket, that's a $764 state tax savings (though it doesn't help with federal).

4. Consider Roth Conversions Before Retirement

If you're still working and in the 6.37% bracket, consider converting some traditional IRA funds to Roth. You'll pay 6.37% state tax now, but future Roth withdrawals are completely tax-free. This is especially valuable if you expect to have high retirement income that would disqualify you from the pension exclusion.

5. Relocate Strategically in Retirement

Many NJ retirees move to Florida, Arizona, or North Carolina to avoid state income tax on their retirement income. But if your income is under the pension exclusion limit ($100k-150k), you may already owe little to no NJ tax, making relocation less urgent. Run the numbers first.

6. Tax-Loss Harvesting

New Jersey taxes capital gains as ordinary income (up to 10.75%). If you have investment losses, use them to offset gains. You can deduct up to $3,000 of net losses per year against ordinary income, saving up to $322/year in state tax (plus federal savings).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the New Jersey state income tax rate for 2026?
New Jersey uses a progressive income tax system with six brackets ranging from 1.4% to 10.75%. The top rate of 10.75% applies to income over $1 million (single) or $5 million (married filing jointly), making it one of the highest state tax rates in the country.
Does New Jersey tax Social Security benefits?
No, New Jersey does not tax Social Security retirement benefits. Additionally, taxpayers with income under certain thresholds can exclude some or all pension and retirement income from state tax.
What is the standard deduction in New Jersey?
New Jersey does not have a standard deduction. However, it offers various exemptions based on filing status and income level: $1,000 for single filers and $2,000 for married filers (both subject to income phase-outs).
Is New Jersey a high-tax state?
Yes, New Jersey is one of the highest-tax states in the nation. With a top income tax rate of 10.75%, high property taxes (averaging $9,284/year), and sales tax of 6.625%, it has one of the highest overall tax burdens in the United States.
Can I deduct property taxes on my New Jersey return?
Yes. Unlike the federal $10,000 SALT cap, New Jersey allows unlimited property tax deductions on your state income tax return. This is significant since NJ has the highest property taxes in the nation (average $9,284/year).
When are New Jersey tax returns due?
New Jersey state tax returns are due on April 15th, the same as federal returns. If you file for a federal extension, NJ automatically grants you an extension to October 15th. However, any tax owed must still be paid by April 15th to avoid interest and penalties.
