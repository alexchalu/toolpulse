Understanding New Jersey Income Tax

New Jersey has a progressive income tax system with six brackets ranging from 1.4% to 10.75%. The state's top rate is among the highest in the nation, but the majority of residents pay much lower effective rates due to graduated brackets and exemptions.

New Jersey Tax Brackets for 2026

New Jersey uses different bracket thresholds for married vs. single filers:

Personal Exemptions (Instead of Standard Deduction)

New Jersey doesn't have a standard deduction. Instead, it offers personal exemptions:

  • Regular exemption: $1,000 per person (taxpayer + spouse)
  • Age 65+ exemption: Additional $1,000 per person
  • Dependent exemption: $1,500 per dependent
  • Blind/disabled exemption: Additional $1,000 per person

Example: Married couple (both 67 years old) with 2 kids: $1,000 + $1,000 (regular) + $1,000 + $1,000 (age 65+) + $1,500 + $1,500 (dependents) = $7,000 in exemptions.

Retirement Income Exclusion

One of NJ's most valuable tax benefits: if your total income is below certain thresholds, ALL retirement income (except Social Security, which is already exempt) is excluded from tax:

  • Single filers: Full exclusion if income under $100,000
  • Married filers: Full exclusion if income under $150,000
  • Applies to pensions, 401(k) distributions, IRA withdrawals, annuities

This makes New Jersey surprisingly tax-friendly for middle-income retirees.

Key Features of New Jersey Tax System

  • Highest top rate: 10.75% (tied with California's 13.3% for highest in the nation)
  • Millionaire's tax: The 10.75% rate only applies above $1 million, affecting fewer than 1% of taxpayers
  • No local income taxes: Unlike PA or OH, NJ cities/counties don't add income taxes
  • Property tax credit: Up to $50 credit for property tax or rent paid (not a deduction)
  • Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC): 40% of federal EITC for lower-income workers

New Jersey vs. Neighboring States

NJ is surrounded by states with very different tax policies. Here's how it compares:

State Tax Structure Top Rate NYC Commuter Note
New Jersey Progressive (6 brackets) 10.75% (over $1M) NJ residents pay NJ tax on all income
New York Progressive (8 brackets) + NYC tax 10.9% state + 3.876% NYC = 14.8% combined NY doesn't tax NJ residents working in NY
Pennsylvania Flat + local taxes 3.07% state + up to 3.9% local = 6.97% Lower rate but local taxes add up
Delaware Progressive (7 brackets) 6.6% (over $60,000) No sales tax but higher income tax than PA

NYC Commuters: If you live in NJ and work in NYC, you pay NJ state tax only (NY doesn't tax non-residents on wages). This can save $3,000-5,000/year vs. living in NYC, even with NJ's high rates.

Who Benefits from Living in New Jersey?

  • NYC Commuters earning $75k-150k: Lower effective rate than NYC (state + city tax)
  • Retirees with income under $100k/$150k: Retirement income exclusion = $0 state tax
  • Lower earners ($20k-40k): 1.4%-3.5% rates are competitive

Who Pays the Most?

  • High earners ($500k-$1M+): 8.97%-10.75% kicks in quickly
  • Combined federal + state: Top earners face 37% federal + 10.75% state = 47.75% marginal rate
  • Property tax consideration: NJ has highest property taxes in the US (avg $9,284/year), which compounds the burden

Tax Planning Strategies for New Jersey Residents

1. Max Out Retirement Contributions

Pre-tax 401(k) and IRA contributions reduce your NJ taxable income at your top marginal rate:

  • 401(k): Up to $23,000 ($30,000 if 50+) for 2026
  • IRA: Up to $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+)

Example: $100k earner in 6.37% bracket saves $1,465 in NJ tax with $23k 401(k) contribution (plus ~$5,060 federal savings = $6,525 total).

2. Claim the Retirement Income Exclusion

If you're retired with income under $100k (single) or $150k (married), structure withdrawals to stay under the threshold. This makes all retirement income tax-free in NJ (except Social Security, which is already exempt).

3. Consider Roth Conversions Before Retirement

Convert traditional IRA/401(k) to Roth before you retire. You'll pay NJ tax on the conversion, but all future Roth withdrawals are tax-free. This is especially valuable if:

  • You're in a lower bracket now (1.4%-5.5%) vs. expected retirement bracket
  • You expect to exceed the $100k/$150k retirement exclusion threshold
  • You want to avoid Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) forcing you over the threshold

4. Take Advantage of Property Tax Credit

NJ offers a $50 property tax or rent credit if you:

  • Paid property tax or rent in NJ
  • Had income under $150,000 (raised from $10,000 in recent years)

It's small, but automatic — claim it on your return.

5. Harvest Capital Losses

NJ follows federal rules for capital gains (taxed as ordinary income). Offset gains with losses to lower your taxable income. You can deduct up to $3,000 in net losses per year, carrying forward the rest.

6. Time Income Around Bracket Thresholds

If you're near a bracket threshold ($75k, $500k, $1M), consider deferring bonuses or accelerating deductions to avoid jumping into the next bracket.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the New Jersey state income tax rate for 2026?
New Jersey uses a progressive income tax system with six brackets ranging from 1.4% to 10.75%. The top rate of 10.75% applies to income over $1 million for married filers and $500,000 for single filers, making it one of the highest state tax rates in the nation.
Does New Jersey tax Social Security benefits?
No, New Jersey does not tax Social Security retirement benefits. Additionally, other retirement income (pensions, 401(k), IRA) is excluded from tax if your total income is below $100,000 (or $150,000 for married couples).
What is the standard deduction in New Jersey?
New Jersey does not have a traditional standard deduction. Instead, it offers personal exemptions: $1,000 per person, plus additional exemptions for seniors ($1,000 extra if 65+), dependents ($1,500 each), and blind/disabled individuals.
Why is New Jersey's income tax so high?
New Jersey has one of the highest top income tax rates (10.75%) because it faces high costs of living, extensive public services, and relies heavily on income and property taxes for revenue. The state also provides substantial education funding and transit infrastructure. However, lower- and middle-income earners pay much lower rates (1.4%-5.5%).
If I work in New York but live in New Jersey, which state do I pay tax to?
You pay New Jersey state tax only. New York does not tax the wages of non-residents (except for convenience-of-employer rules in some cases). This is a major benefit for NJ residents commuting to NYC, saving thousands vs. living in NYC and paying both NY state and NYC local taxes.
When are New Jersey state tax returns due?
New Jersey state tax returns are due on April 15th, the same deadline as federal returns. If you file a federal extension to October 15th, New Jersey automatically grants you an extension as well (though any tax owed must still be paid by April 15th to avoid penalties).
