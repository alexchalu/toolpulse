Understanding New Jersey Income Tax

New Jersey operates one of the most progressive income tax systems in the United States, with seven brackets ranging from 1.4% to 10.75%. The state's high top rate is designed to tax wealthy earners heavily while keeping rates relatively low for middle-income residents.

Personal Exemptions (No Standard Deduction)

New Jersey doesn't have a standard deduction. Instead, you claim personal exemptions:

  • Personal Exemption: $1,000 per person (taxpayer + spouse)
  • Dependent Exemption: $1,500 per qualifying dependent
  • Age 65+ or Blind: Additional $1,000 exemption per person
  • Veteran/Disabled: Additional exemption (up to $6,000)

Example: Married couple (both under 65) with two children = $2,000 (personal) + $3,000 (dependents) = $5,000 in exemptions.

Key Features of New Jersey Tax System

  • 10.75% Top Rate: One of the highest in the nation (tied with NY's top rate, higher than CA's effective rate for most earners).
  • No Social Security Tax: Social Security benefits are 100% exempt from NJ tax.
  • Retirement Income Exclusion: Pensions, 401(k)s, and IRAs are fully exempt if income is under $100k (single) or $150k (married).
  • Property Tax Deduction: Up to $15,000 deduction for property taxes paid (important given NJ's nation-leading property tax rates).
  • Earned Income Tax Credit: 40% of the federal EITC (can be refundable).
  • Child and Dependent Care Credit: Available for working parents.

NYC Commuter Considerations

If you live in NJ and work in NYC, you face unique tax situations:

  • NJ Resident Credit: You get a credit for taxes paid to NY, so you don't pay double tax on the same income.
  • Effective Rate: You generally pay the higher of the two states' rates. Since NJ and NY have similar top rates, you'll pay close to the full amount to one or both.
  • NYC Tax: If you work in NYC, you may also owe NYC local tax (3.078%-3.876%) if you're a NYC resident, but not if you're a NJ resident working in NYC.
  • Property Tax Trade-Off: Many NYC workers move to NJ for lower housing costs but face NJ's high property taxes ($9k-$15k/year average).

Moving to New Jersey? What to Know

  • Residency Rules: You're a NJ resident if you maintain a permanent home in NJ or spend 183+ days in the state.
  • Part-Year Filing: If you move mid-year, file part-year returns for both states, allocating income by days of residency.
  • Retiree Appeal: NJ is attractive for retirees with income under $100k/$150k (full pension exemption + no Social Security tax).
  • High Earner Exodus: The 10.75% top rate drives some high earners to FL (0%) or PA (3.07% flat).

Tax Planning Strategies for New Jersey Residents

1. Maximize Retirement Contributions

Traditional 401(k) and IRA contributions reduce your NJ taxable income. For 2026:

  • 401(k): Up to $23,000 ($30,000 if age 50+)
  • IRA: Up to $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+)

At NJ's 6.37% rate (most middle-income earners), a $23,000 401(k) contribution saves ~$1,465 in state tax, plus federal savings.

2. Claim All Eligible Exemptions

Don't miss exemptions:

  • $1,500 per dependent (children, elderly parents)
  • Additional $1,000 if you or your spouse is 65+
  • Additional $1,000 if blind
  • Veteran exemptions if you served

3. Take the Property Tax Deduction

NJ property taxes average $9,000-$10,000/year (highest in the nation). You can deduct up to $15,000 of property taxes on your NJ return. At a 6.37% marginal rate, a $10,000 deduction saves $637.

4. Retirement Income Exclusion (If Eligible)

If your total income is under $100,000 (single) or $150,000 (married), ALL your retirement income (pensions, 401(k) withdrawals, IRA distributions) is excluded from NJ tax. This is huge — a retiree with $80,000 in pension income pays $0 NJ tax.

5. College Savings (NJ 529 Plan)

New Jersey does NOT offer a state tax deduction for 529 contributions (unlike most states). However, the account grows tax-free and withdrawals for qualified education expenses are tax-free at both federal and state levels.

6. Timing Capital Gains

NJ taxes long-term capital gains as ordinary income (no preferential rate like the federal 15%-20%). If you're in the 10.75% bracket and selling stock, consider:

  • Deferring sales to a year with lower income
  • Harvesting losses to offset gains
  • Donating appreciated stock to charity (deduction + avoid tax)

7. Consider Relocating (High Earners)

If you're earning $500k+ and paying 10.75% NJ tax, moving to a no-tax state like FL, TX, or NV saves tens of thousands per year. Example: $1M earner saves ~$107,500/year by moving to Florida. But factor in property taxes, cost of living, and quality of life.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the New Jersey state income tax rate for 2026?
New Jersey uses a progressive income tax system with seven brackets ranging from 1.4% to 10.75%. The top rate of 10.75% applies to income over $1 million for married filers and $500,000 for single filers, making it one of the highest state tax rates in the nation.
Does New Jersey tax Social Security benefits?
No, New Jersey does not tax Social Security retirement benefits. Additionally, retirement income (pensions, 401(k)s, IRAs) is fully exempt for taxpayers with total income under $100,000 (single) or $150,000 (married).
Does New Jersey have a standard deduction?
No, New Jersey does not have a standard deduction. Instead, the state offers personal exemptions and dependent exemptions that reduce your taxable income. For 2026, the personal exemption is $1,000 per person, with additional exemptions based on income and age.
What are the property tax deductions in New Jersey?
New Jersey offers a property tax deduction (up to $15,000) or credit (up to $50) depending on income. The deduction is available for homeowners and tenants. Given NJ's high property taxes (averaging $9,000-$10,000/year), this can provide significant relief.
When are New Jersey state tax returns due?
New Jersey state tax returns are due on April 15th, the same deadline as federal returns. If you file a federal extension to October 15th, New Jersey automatically grants you an extension as well.
How does NJ tax compare to NY for NYC commuters?
NJ residents working in NYC pay tax to both states but receive a credit from NJ for taxes paid to NY, avoiding double taxation. You effectively pay the higher of the two states' rates. NJ's top rate (10.75%) is nearly identical to NY's (10.9%), so the difference is minimal. However, NJ residents don't pay NYC's local tax (3%-4%).
