## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Investment Thesis

76 Central Avenue presents as a **distressed single-tenant industrial land parcel** with significant complexity arising from tenant credit deterioration, a heavily amended lease with full rent abatement through expiration, and material environmental conditions including four (4) Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs) and one (1) Controlled Recognized Environmental Condition (CREC). The property is being marketed by JLL and warrants deep scrutiny before any acquisition commitment.

### Critical Findings Summary

1. **Tenant is effectively non-performing.** The Fourth Amendment (January 2025) acknowledges $414,456.80 in arrears and abates **all Fixed Rent** through the lease expiration in August 2032. The tenant is paying only a $2,252.48/month installment toward arrears plus Additional Charges (taxes, insurance). **No fixed rent revenue** will be generated for the remaining ~7.5 years of the term.

2. **Environmental liability is material.** The Phase I ESA identifies six RECs including historic fill across the entire site, residual railroad contamination, petroleum staining, exposed chemical storage, and a historical spill. A CREC for groundwater contamination (Classification Exception Area) extends onto the property from the adjacent site. The property abuts the Diamond Alkali Superfund Site (Passaic River) — one of the most contaminated Superfund sites in the United States.

3. **Title is complex.** The property straddles Hudson County (Kearny) and Essex County (Newark) — with the Essex County portion lying entirely under the Passaic River and having no recorded chain of title. Twenty-six (26) Schedule B exceptions exist, including riparian rights, railroad rights-of-way, tidelands claims, and federal navigation controls.

4. **Landlord has secured termination optionality.** The Fourth Amendment grants the landlord a unilateral 90-day termination right if a replacement tenant is found, plus 24-hour marketing access rights — indicating the current owner is already positioning for tenant replacement or property disposition.

5. **Redevelopment potential is constrained.** SKI-S zoning limits uses primarily to warehouse/distribution (with exceptions excluding truck terminals and motor freight from SKI-S that are permitted in SKI-N), all operations must be in completely enclosed buildings, and coastal zone regulations add permitting complexity.

#### REC 1: Historic Fill

**Description:** EDR topographic map review and NJDEP GEOWEB confirm that **historic fill is mapped across the entire site**. The site is located in an area of historical wetlands (elevation ~9 feet MSL) that was filled for development.

**Significance:** If future development requires excavation and off-site disposal of soil, soil testing and special handling will be required. Historic fill frequently contains construction debris, industrial waste, coal ash, and other contaminants. If soil is left in place, it may remain under a deed notice (institutional control) and potential Classification Exception Area (groundwater institutional control).

**Recommended Action:** Subsurface investigation prior to any development involving excavation. Phase II ESA required to characterize fill material.

#### REC 2: Historical Railroad

**Description:** EDR Historical Aerial Photographs reveal evidence of a historical rail line traversing the site (Newark & New York Railroad, later Consolidated Rail Corporation). Abandoned rail line remnants were observed during the July 2021 site inspection along the south and northeast of the property.

**Significance:** Residual railroad components (ties, ballast, track) may remain below grade. Railroad ties are commonly treated with creosote (a coal tar derivative containing polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons — PAHs). Railroad ballast frequently contains heavy metals and petroleum compounds.

**Recommended Action:** Subsurface soil evaluation to determine absence/presence of railroad components and resulting contamination.

#### REC 3: Historical Loading/Unloading Areas

**Description:** Apparent loading and unloading areas were observed in EDR Historic Aerial Photographs in association with the railroad operations. These areas historically served as transfer points for goods and materials.

**Significance:** Loading/unloading areas are common sources of spills, releases, and soil contamination from the handling of materials including petroleum products, solvents, metals, and other hazardous substances.

**Recommended Action:** Subsurface soil evaluation in locations where loading/unloading may have occurred.

#### REC 4: Staining/Discharge — Petroleum

**Description:** Evidence of **petroleum discharge** was observed in the **westernmost portion** of the site during the July 2021 site reconnaissance.

**Significance:** Active petroleum staining indicates current or recent releases to the environment. The western portion of the site is closest to the Passaic River (~65 feet), creating heightened risk of migration to surface water — including the Diamond Alkali Superfund stretch of the river.

**Recommended Action:** Subsurface soil evaluation and potential groundwater monitoring in the western site area.

#### REC 5: Exposed Chemical Storage

**Description:** Several **five (5)-gallon containers, partially filled drums, and one (1) 250-gallon container** were observed in the western portion of the site, **situated directly on grass, gravel, or cracked asphalt** (no containment). Staining was observed on some containers/drums.

**Significance:** Chemical storage without proper containment (concrete pads, secondary containment, berms) on permeable surfaces (grass, gravel, cracked asphalt) creates direct pathways for chemical release to soil and potentially groundwater. The 250-gallon tote contained an unknown liquid.

**Recommended Action:** Subsurface soil evaluation in chemical storage areas. Identification and proper disposal/containment of stored chemicals.

## 12. RECOMMENDATION

### 12.1 Investment Decision Framework

This property presents a classic **"basis play"** — the opportunity to acquire at a deep discount to replacement cost and intrinsic land value due to tenant distress and environmental complexity that most institutional buyers will avoid.

### 12.2 Conditions for Proceeding

An acquisition should proceed **only** if the following conditions are met:

1. **Phase II ESA:** Commission a comprehensive Phase II Environmental Site Assessment addressing all six RECs and the CREC. The Phase II must include subsurface soil sampling, groundwater sampling, historic fill characterization, and vapor intrusion assessment. **Budget: $75,000–$150,000.** This is non-negotiable.

2. **Remediation Cost Estimate:** Obtain a Licensed Site Remediation Professional (LSRP) opinion on estimated remediation costs under three scenarios: (a) continued current use, (b) redevelopment with excavation, (c) institutional/engineering controls only. Negotiate environmental indemnity or price reduction accordingly.

3. **Tenant Financial Review:** Demand the audited financial statements required under Section 7 of the Fourth Amendment. If the tenant is insolvent, plan for default and vacancy.

4. **Title Clearance:** Complete the riparian/tidelands search. Verify ConRail/successor railroad abandonment status. Confirm satisfaction mechanics for the Aresif Lenders mortgage.

5. **Zoning Confirmation:** Obtain final PZR report with municipal information. Confirm current use legality (outdoor storage in SKI-S). Verify site plan approval status.

6. **Price Negotiation:** Given the combination of risk factors, an appropriate acquisition price should reflect:
   - A significant discount to the 2021 acquisition price of $13,750,000
   - A discount for environmental risk (at minimum the estimated remediation cost)
   - A discount for vacancy/re-leasing risk
   - A discount for tenant credit risk

### 12.5 Key Caveats

1. **Phase II results are determinative.** If Phase II reveals contamination requiring $5M+ in remediation, the economics shift dramatically. Do not close without Phase II results.
2. **Tenant cooperation is uncertain.** The Fourth Amendment requires confidentiality, and the tenant may resist cooperation with marketing efforts despite contractual obligations.

4. **PFOA/PFAS is an evolving regulatory area.** New Jersey has been aggressive on PFAS regulation. Even though the LSRP attributes PFOA to an off-site source, regulatory changes could impose monitoring or remediation obligations on the property owner.
