### Critical Title Issues

1. **Environmental Restrictions (Exception 18 & 19):** The Conservation Restriction and Environmental Restrictions are tied to the Monsanto Company ECRA Cases (Case Nos. 87958 and 89711). A restricted-use No Further Action letter was issued by NJDEP in 1992 and a Soil Remedial Action Permit ("Soil RAP") was issued in 2014. Two areas of the property are subject to deed notices, engineering controls, and biennial certification requirements. These are the **responsibility of the Landlord** per the Doka lease (Section 36.13).

2. **Railroad Easement (Exception 12):** The property contains existing/former rail lines. The Phase I ESA identified contamination associated with these rail lines as a recognized environmental condition. The easement reservation for railroad purposes must be reviewed to confirm operational impact.

3. **Riparian/Waterway Rights (Exception 20):** The Rahway River and Black Brook form property boundaries. While the PSEG lease flood disclosure states the property is **not** in a Special Flood Hazard Area or Moderate Risk Flood Hazard Area per current FEMA maps, the riparian rights exception should be evaluated in the context of future flood risk, particularly given NJ climate change flood risk disclosures.

# 13. MARKET ANALYSIS

## 13.1 Northern NJ Industrial Market

The 251 Monroe Avenue property benefits from its location in one of the tightest industrial markets in the United States:

- **Market:** Northern New Jersey / New York Metro industrial corridor
- **Submarket:** Union County, within the Route 22 industrial corridor
- **Proximity:** ~20 miles from Manhattan; ~15 miles from Newark Liberty International Airport; ~10 miles from Port Newark-Elizabeth
- **Access:** Route 22 (major east-west arterial), Garden State Parkway, NJ Turnpike

## 13.2 Industrial Outdoor Storage (IOS) Market

IOS has emerged as one of the most sought-after industrial real estate sub-sectors:

- **Supply Constraints:** IOS properties are nearly impossible to develop new due to zoning restrictions, environmental regulations, NIMBYism, and the scarcity of large parcels in infill locations
- **Demand Drivers:** E-commerce logistics, utility infrastructure investment, construction activity, school bus/fleet operations, last-mile delivery staging
- **Rental Growth:** IOS rents in the NY/NJ metro have increased 50–100% over the past 5 years as institutional capital has entered the sector
- **Cap Rate Compression:** IOS assets in infill markets have traded at 4.5%–6.0% cap rates, down from 7.0%–8.0% five years ago

## 13.3 Comparable Rent Analysis

Doka's current rent of $17,000/acre/month ($289,000/month for 19 acres) reflects below-market pricing for the northern NJ IOS market, where comparable assets command $20,000–$30,000/acre/month. The June 2027 step-up to $20,500/acre/month begins to close this gap, with 3% annual escalations moving toward market rate over time.

## 13.4 Replacement Cost Analysis

Given the 43.4-acre site and 261,171 SF of improvements:
- **Land Value Estimate:** $1.5M–$2.5M per acre (infill NJ IOS) = $65M–$109M
- **Improvement Replacement:** $100–$150/SF × 261,171 SF = $26M–$39M
- **Total Replacement Cost Estimate:** $91M–$148M

The property's replacement cost significantly exceeds the implied acquisition value, providing a substantial margin of safety.
