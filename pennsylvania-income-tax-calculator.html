Understanding Pennsylvania Income Tax in 2026

Pennsylvania operates one of the simplest state income tax systems in the United States. With a flat 3.07% tax rate on taxable income, PA doesn't have progressive tax brackets like most states. This means whether you earn $30,000 or $300,000, your state tax rate remains constant at 3.07%.

Key Pennsylvania Tax Facts for 2026

  • State tax rate: 3.07% flat (no brackets)
  • No standard deduction: PA doesn't offer standard or itemized deductions
  • Tax-exempt income: Retirement income, Social Security, unemployment benefits
  • Local taxes: Most municipalities levy additional Earned Income Tax (1-3.84%)
  • Filing requirement: Any PA resident with taxable income must file

Pennsylvania's Flat Tax Structure

Pennsylvania is one of only eight states with a flat income tax rate. The 3.07% rate applies uniformly to all taxable income categories:

  • Wages and salaries - Income from employment (W-2)
  • Business income - Self-employment and Schedule C income
  • Interest income - From bank accounts and bonds
  • Dividends - From stock investments
  • Capital gains - From sale of assets and property
  • Rental income - From investment properties
  • Royalties - From intellectual property and mineral rights
  • Gambling/lottery winnings - Pennsylvania lottery winnings are actually exempt (unique among taxable categories)

Tax-Exempt Income in Pennsylvania

Pennsylvania is exceptionally generous with retirement income exemptions, making it one of the most tax-friendly states for retirees. The following income sources are completely exempt from PA state income tax:

Retirement Income (100% Exempt)

  • Social Security benefits - Fully exempt regardless of income level
  • 401(k) and 403(b) distributions - No PA tax on withdrawals
  • Traditional IRA distributions - Tax-free at state level
  • Roth IRA distributions - Already federally tax-free, also PA exempt
  • Pension payments - Public and private pensions fully exempt
  • Annuity income - From qualified retirement annuities
  • Military retirement pay - 100% PA tax-exempt
  • Railroad retirement benefits - Fully exempt

Other Exempt Income

  • Unemployment compensation - Not subject to PA state tax (but federally taxable)
  • Workers' compensation - Benefits fully exempt
  • Disability payments - From employer-funded plans
  • Life insurance proceeds - Death benefits are tax-free
  • Child support - Not taxable income
  • Gifts and inheritances - No PA income tax (but PA inheritance tax may apply separately)
  • PA lottery winnings - State lottery prizes exempt (federal tax still applies)

Retiree Tax Advantage

A Pennsylvania retiree living solely on Social Security ($30,000) and 401(k) distributions ($50,000) would pay $0 in PA state income tax - all $80,000 is tax-exempt. This makes PA highly attractive for retirement compared to states like California (up to 13.3% on retirement income) or New York (up to 10.9%).

Pennsylvania Local Earned Income Tax (EIT)

In addition to the 3.07% state tax, Pennsylvania municipalities (cities, townships, boroughs) and school districts levy their own Earned Income Tax (EIT). This is where PA's tax system becomes more complex.

Work Location vs. Residence

Pennsylvania's local EIT has a unique split for workers who live and work in different municipalities:

  • Resident tax: Portion of EIT paid to your home municipality
  • Non-resident tax: Portion paid to your work municipality
  • Combined rate: Total EIT remains the same regardless of split

Example: You live in Allentown (1.5% EIT) but work in Bethlehem (1.5% EIT). You pay 1% to Allentown (residence) and 0.5% to Bethlehem (work location) - total still 1.5%.

Who Must File a Pennsylvania Tax Return?

You must file a PA-40 (Personal Income Tax Return) if you meet any of these criteria:

  • PA resident with any taxable income (wages, business income, interest, dividends, capital gains)
  • Non-resident with PA-source income (worked in PA, rental property in PA)
  • Part-year resident who moved to or from PA during the tax year
  • Self-employed individuals with net profit from PA business

You do NOT need to file a PA return if your only income is from exempt sources (Social Security, retirement distributions, unemployment compensation) - but you may still owe local EIT if you have earned income.

Tax Forgiveness Credit (Most Common)

The PA Tax Forgiveness Credit is designed to eliminate or reduce state income tax for low-income residents. For 2026, eligibility thresholds are:

  • Single filer: Income under $9,500 - full forgiveness; up to $35,000 - partial credit
  • Married filing jointly: Income under $18,500 - full forgiveness; up to $41,000 - partial credit
  • Head of household: Income under $15,000 - full forgiveness; up to $38,000 - partial credit

This credit is automatically calculated when you file PA-40 Form - no separate application required.

Pennsylvania Reciprocal Tax Agreements

Pennsylvania has reciprocal income tax agreements with six neighboring states. If you are a resident of one of these states working in Pennsylvania, you do not pay PA income tax on wages:

  • Indiana - IN residents working in PA pay IN tax only
  • Maryland - MD residents working in PA pay MD tax only
  • New Jersey - NJ residents working in PA pay NJ tax only
  • Ohio - OH residents working in PA pay OH tax only
  • Virginia - VA residents working in PA pay VA tax only
  • West Virginia - WV residents working in PA pay WV tax only

How it works: File Form REV-419 (Employee's Nonwithholding Application Certificate) with your PA employer to stop PA withholding. You'll pay tax only to your home state.

Important: This applies only to wages from employment. If you earn business income, rental income, or other PA-source income as a non-resident, you still owe PA tax on those amounts.

Remote Work and Pennsylvania Taxes

The rise of remote work has created new tax scenarios for Pennsylvania residents:

PA Resident Working Remotely for Out-of-State Employer

  • PA state tax: You owe 3.07% on all income (work is performed in PA)
  • Local EIT: Owed to your PA municipality where you live
  • Other state tax: Generally not owed unless you travel to that state for work
  • Withholding: Ask employer to withhold PA taxes; if not, pay quarterly estimated taxes

Out-of-State Resident Working Remotely for PA Employer

  • PA tax: Not owed if work is performed entirely outside PA (unless reciprocal state)
  • Your state tax: Owed to your home state
  • Exception: If you travel to PA offices occasionally, that portion may be PA-taxable

PA "Convenience of the Employer" Rule

Pennsylvania does not have a "convenience of the employer" rule (unlike New York). This means:

  • If you work remotely from another state for your own convenience (not employer requirement), you still pay tax to your home state, not PA
  • PA only taxes income for work physically performed in Pennsylvania
  • This makes PA more favorable for employers with remote workers compared to NY

Self-Employment and Business Income

If you're self-employed or own a business in Pennsylvania, your tax obligations are more complex:

State Income Tax (3.07%)

  • Applies to net profit from Schedule C (sole proprietorship) or pass-through entities (LLC, S-corp, partnership)
  • Calculated after business expenses and deductions
  • No separate PA business income tax - flows through to personal return

Local Earned Income Tax (1-3.84%)

  • Applies to business income in addition to state tax
  • Rate determined by your business location (where work is performed)
  • If home-based business, use your residence address for EIT rate

Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

If you expect to owe more than $8,000 in PA tax for the year, you must make quarterly estimated payments:

  • April 15, 2026 - Q1 payment (25% of annual estimate)
  • June 16, 2026 - Q2 payment
  • September 15, 2026 - Q3 payment
  • January 15, 2027 - Q4 payment

Underpayment penalty: If you don't pay at least 90% of your annual tax or 100% of prior year's tax through withholding/estimates, you may owe penalties.

Property Tax and Sales Tax (Not Income Tax)

While not income taxes, these are significant Pennsylvania tax obligations:

Property Tax

  • County and municipal taxes: Average effective rate 1.36% of home value (14th highest nationally)
  • School district taxes: Separate property tax for local schools (varies widely)
  • Homestead exemption: Available for primary residences (reduces assessed value)
  • Senior citizen programs: Property tax rebate program for seniors 65+ with income under $35,000

Sales Tax

  • State rate: 6% on most goods and services
  • Philadelphia: Additional 2% local sales tax (total 8%)
  • Allegheny County (Pittsburgh): Additional 1% local sales tax (total 7%)
  • Exemptions: Groceries, prescription drugs, clothing, textbooks

Tax Reduction Strategies for Pennsylvania Residents

1. Maximize Retirement Contributions

While PA doesn't allow deductions for retirement contributions (unlike federal taxes), building tax-deferred retirement accounts creates future PA tax-free income. Every dollar in your 401(k) or IRA will be withdrawn tax-free at the state level in retirement.

2. Structure Investment Income Wisely

Pennsylvania taxes interest and dividends at 3.07%, but capital gains on appreciated assets are also taxed. Consider:

  • Municipal bonds: PA municipal bond interest is exempt from both state and federal taxes
  • Long-term holdings: Defer capital gains by holding investments longer
  • Tax-loss harvesting: Offset capital gains with capital losses to reduce PA tax

3. Optimize Work Location for Local EIT

If you have flexibility in work location (remote work, multiple offices), working from a municipality with lower EIT can save significantly:

  • Philadelphia resident working remotely from home: 3.8398% EIT
  • Same person working from office in suburban Montgomery County: ~1.5% EIT
  • Annual savings on $100,000 income: $2,340

4. Plan Relocations Around Tax Year

If moving to/from Pennsylvania, timing matters:

  • PA taxes based on residency status, not full-year
  • Moving out before high-income events (bonus, stock sale) can save PA tax
  • Part-year residents file PA-40 with Schedule G allocation

5. Claim All Eligible Credits

  • Tax Forgiveness Credit: Don't overlook if income is borderline eligible
  • Educational expenses: While no state deduction, federal credits reduce overall tax burden
  • Charitable contributions: Federal itemized deduction (though no PA benefit)

Pennsylvania Inheritance Tax (Separate from Income Tax)

Pennsylvania is one of few states with an inheritance tax (separate from estate tax). When you inherit assets from a PA decedent:

Beneficiary Relationship Tax Rate
Spouse 0% (exempt)
Children, grandchildren, parents 4.5%
Siblings 12%
All other heirs (nieces, nephews, friends) 15%

Exemptions: Life insurance proceeds, retirement accounts passing to spouse, and property left to charities are exempt from PA inheritance tax.

This is not income tax: Inherited money is not taxable income for PA purposes - you pay the one-time inheritance tax when you receive the asset, then it's yours tax-free.

  • Schedule C: Profit or loss from business
  • Schedule D: Capital gains and losses

Common Pennsylvania Tax Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Forgetting local EIT: Many filers correctly calculate 3.07% state tax but overlook their local earned income tax
  2. Not claiming retirement income exemption: Retirees sometimes report 401(k)/IRA distributions as taxable when they're PA-exempt
  3. Misunderstanding reciprocal agreements: NJ/NY residents working in PA often overpay by not filing exemption certificates
  4. Missing quarterly estimated payments: Self-employed filers face penalties if they don't pay throughout the year
  5. Incorrect work location for EIT: Remote workers should use home address, not employer's office location
  6. Not separating PA lottery winnings: PA lottery is exempt, but other gambling winnings are taxable
  7. Overlooking tax forgiveness credit: Low-income filers miss automatic credit by not filing PA-40

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Pennsylvania state income tax rate for 2026?

Pennsylvania has a flat income tax rate of 3.07% for 2026. This rate applies to all taxable income regardless of filing status or income level. PA is one of only eight states with a flat tax structure.

What income is exempt from PA state tax?

Pennsylvania exempts several income sources from state tax:

  • Social Security benefits
  • Retirement account distributions (401k, IRA, pension)
  • Unemployment compensation
  • Life insurance proceeds
  • Child support
  • Workers' compensation
  • Disability payments from employer plans
  • Pennsylvania lottery/gambling winnings

Note: While exempt from state tax, some income (like wages) may still be subject to local Earned Income Tax (EIT).

What is Pennsylvania local Earned Income Tax (EIT)?

Pennsylvania local Earned Income Tax (EIT) is a municipal tax levied by cities, townships, boroughs, and school districts. Rates typically range from 1% to 3.8398% depending on location. Philadelphia has the highest rate at 3.8398% (city wage tax). Pittsburgh's combined rate is 3.00%.

EIT applies only to wages, salaries, and business income - not to retirement income, interest, or dividends. Your employer typically withholds this tax automatically.

Do I have to pay PA tax if I work remotely for an out-of-state employer?

If you are a Pennsylvania resident working remotely for an out-of-state employer, you must pay PA state income tax (3.07%) on your wages because the work is performed in Pennsylvania. You may also owe local EIT to your municipality.

However, PA has reciprocal agreements with Indiana, Maryland, New Jersey, Ohio, Virginia, and West Virginia. Residents of those states working in PA do not pay PA tax on wages - they pay only to their home state.

Is Social Security income taxed in Pennsylvania?

No. Pennsylvania is one of the most tax-friendly states for retirees. Social Security benefits are 100% exempt from PA state income tax, regardless of your income level. This exemption also extends to all retirement account distributions (401k, IRA, pensions).

How do I find my local EIT rate?

To find your specific local Earned Income Tax rate:

  1. Visit the PA Department of Community & Economic Development
  2. Enter your municipality name or county
  3. View your combined resident and non-resident EIT rates

Alternatively, check your pay stub - your employer should be withholding local EIT and it will be itemized there.

Does Pennsylvania have a standard deduction?

No. Pennsylvania does not allow standard deductions or itemized deductions. The 3.07% tax rate applies directly to your taxable income without any adjustments for deductions (except specific exemptions like retirement income).

This differs from federal taxes, where you can claim a standard deduction ($14,600 for singles in 2026). At the state level, PA taxes gross income minus exempt categories.

When is Pennsylvania tax due?

Pennsylvania tax returns and payments are due on April 15, 2026 for the 2025 tax year (same as federal deadline). If you file for an extension, your return is due by October 15, 2026, but any tax owed must still be paid by April 15 to avoid penalties.

Self-employed individuals must also make quarterly estimated tax payments on April 15, June 16, September 15, and January 15.

Can I deduct mortgage interest on my PA tax return?

No. Pennsylvania does not allow itemized deductions, including mortgage interest, property taxes, or charitable contributions. These deductions apply only on your federal return.

PA taxes your income at a flat 3.07% without any deductions - it's a much simpler system than federal taxes, but offers fewer opportunities to reduce your tax bill through deductions.
