How Many Calories Should I Eat?

Your complete guide to calculating daily calorie needs for weight loss, maintenance, or muscle gain — with a free calculator.

📅 Updated March 2026⏱ 7 min read🏋️ Health

Quick answer: The average adult needs 1,600–2,400 calories/day (women) or 2,000–3,000 calories/day (men) depending on age, height, weight, and activity level. Use our calculator below for your exact number.

Understanding Your Calorie Needs

Your daily calorie need is called your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE). It's made up of three components:

Basal Metabolic Rate

Calories burned at rest

15-30%
Physical Activity

10%
Thermic Effect

For Weight Loss

To lose weight, you need a calorie deficit — eating fewer calories than your body burns. A safe deficit is 500-750 calories/day, which produces about 1-1.5 pounds of fat loss per week.

⚠️ Important: Never go below 1,200 calories/day (women) or 1,500 calories/day (men) without medical supervision. Extreme restriction slows metabolism and causes muscle loss.

For Muscle Gain

To build muscle, eat a calorie surplus of 250-500 calories/day above your TDEE, combined with resistance training. This supports muscle growth while minimizing fat gain.

The Mifflin-St Jeor Formula

The most accurate formula for estimating BMR (and therefore TDEE):

Men: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) - (5 × age) + 5

Women: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) - (5 × age) - 161

Then multiply by your activity factor: Sedentary (×1.2), Light exercise (×1.375), Moderate (×1.55), Very active (×1.725), Extra active (×1.9).

Frequently Asked Questions

Is 1,200 calories a day enough?

For most adults, 1,200 calories is the minimum recommended for women and is too low for most men. At this level, it's difficult to get adequate nutrition. Consult a doctor before going this low.

How accurate are calorie calculators?

Online calculators are estimates within 10-15% of your actual needs. They're a great starting point — then adjust based on real results over 2-4 weeks.

Should I count calories?

Calorie counting works well for short-term goals but isn't necessary long-term. Many people find success with portion control and mindful eating instead.
