🚨 California DUI Laws & Penalties

California takes DUI offenses seriously. Here's what you need to know about California's DUI laws:

  • BAC Limit: 0.08 (standard legal limit)
  • Lookback Period: 10 years — prior DUIs count within this window
  • Court Fines (1st offense): $390-$5,000
  • Jail Time: 96 hrs - 1 year
  • License Suspension (1st): 6 months
  • Ignition Interlock: Required (all)
  • Felony DUI: 4th in 10 years
  • SR-22 Insurance Required: Yes, for 3 years minimum

💰 Complete DUI Cost Breakdown in California

A DUI in California costs far more than just the court fine. The total financial impact typically ranges from $15,000-$25,000 for a first offense. Here's where the money goes:

  • Court Fines & Penalties: $390-$5,000 — The base fine, plus surcharges, assessments, and fees that can double or triple the stated amount
  • Attorney Fees: $3,000 - $25,000 — A private DUI attorney in California typically charges $3,000-$10,000 for a plea deal, or $10,000-$25,000 for a trial
  • SR-22 Insurance: $2,500-$4,500/yr — Required for 3+ years, adding $5,000-$12,000 to your total insurance costs
  • Ignition Interlock Device: $1,200 - $3,000/year — Required (all). Installation: $70-$150. Monthly monitoring: $60-$80
  • DUI Classes: $500 - $2,500 — Mandatory alcohol education programs in California
  • License Reinstatement: $100 - $500 — Fee to get your license back after suspension
  • Bail/Bond: $500 - $10,000 — Depends on BAC level and circumstances
  • Towing & Impound: $300 - $2,000 — Vehicle towing and impound fees
  • Lost Income: $1,000 - $10,000+ — Missed work for court dates, classes, and jail time

🛡️ 5 Steps After a DUI Arrest in California

  • 1. Request a DMV Hearing: You typically have 10-30 days to request a hearing to contest your license suspension in California. Missing this deadline means automatic suspension.
  • 2. Hire a DUI Attorney: An experienced California DUI lawyer can potentially reduce charges, negotiate plea deals, or identify procedural errors that could dismiss the case.
  • 3. Attend All Court Dates: Missing a court date in California will result in a bench warrant and additional charges. Mark every date on your calendar.
  • 4. Complete Required Programs: California requires completion of alcohol education or treatment programs. Start early — courts look favorably on proactive completion.
  • 5. Get SR-22 Insurance: Contact your insurance company or shop for new coverage. SR-22 filing is required in California for at least 3 years. Compare rates — they vary significantly between insurers.

❓ California DUI FAQ

How much does a DUI cost in California?
The total cost of a first-offense DUI in California typically ranges from $15,000-$25,000, including fines ($390-$5,000), attorney fees ($3,000-$10,000), SR-22 insurance increases ($2,500-$4,500/yr), DUI classes, and other expenses.
How long does a DUI stay on your record in California?
California has a 10-year lookback period for DUI offenses. This means prior DUIs within 10 years will be counted when determining penalties for subsequent offenses. A DUI conviction typically stays on your driving record for 5-10 years.
Do I need SR-22 insurance after a DUI in California?
Yes. California requires SR-22 insurance filing for a minimum of 3 years after a DUI conviction. SR-22 is a certificate proving you carry minimum liability coverage. Expect to pay $2,500-$4,500/yr more per year for insurance with an SR-22 filing.
Can I get a hardship license in California after a DUI?
California may offer a restricted/hardship license for work or essential travel during your suspension period. Requirements typically include installing an ignition interlock device and completing certain conditions. Consult a California DUI attorney for specific eligibility.
Is a first DUI a felony in California?
A first-offense DUI in California is typically a misdemeanor. It becomes a felony at: 4th in 10 years. However, any DUI involving serious injury or death can be charged as a felony regardless of offense number.
What is the BAC limit in California?
The legal BAC limit in California is 0.08 for drivers 21 and over. For commercial drivers, the limit is 0.04. For drivers under 21, California has a zero-tolerance policy (typically 0.02 or lower).
