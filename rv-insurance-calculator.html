What types of RV insurance are available?
RV insurance comes in several forms: liability only (cheapest, covers damage to others), comprehensive + collision (covers your RV too), and full-timer's coverage (treats the RV like a home). Specialized add-ons include vacation liability, emergency expense coverage, personal belongings coverage, and roadside assistance with RV-specific towing.
What's the difference between full-time and recreational RV insurance?
Recreational policies assume the RV is a vehicle you use occasionally. Full-timer's insurance treats your RV as your primary residence — it includes personal liability (like homeowners), higher personal belongings coverage, and loss of use benefits. Full-time coverage costs 30-40% more but is essential if you live in your RV.
Do I need insurance for a travel trailer?
Your tow vehicle's liability coverage extends to the trailer while being towed, but it does NOT cover damage to the trailer itself, theft while parked, or comprehensive/collision. A separate travel trailer policy is highly recommended — they're relatively cheap ($300-$800/year) and protect a significant asset.
How can I save on RV insurance?
Bundle with auto insurance (10-20% off), take an RV safety course, increase your deductible, install anti-theft devices, store in a secure facility when not in use, and ask about pay-per-mile if you don't drive much. Also, Class B vans and travel trailers are significantly cheaper to insure than Class A motorhomes.
Does auto insurance cover my RV?
Standard auto insurance does NOT adequately cover RVs. RVs have unique risks: they're larger, more expensive, contain living quarters, and may include appliances and personal belongings. You need a dedicated RV policy, especially for motorized RVs (Class A/B/C). Towable trailers need their own coverage separate from your tow vehicle.
